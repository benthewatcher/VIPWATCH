## 2. SERVICES PAGE

### Page header

**Seven departments. One bench.**

Every commission moves through one or more of these. Some watches need a single touch. Others pass through every department before they leave.

I do the work myself. The departments are a way of describing it — not a production line.

---

### Department 1 — Engraving

**Engraving.**

Hand engraving on bezels, case backs, lugs, rotors and bridges. Bulino, anglage-cut lettering, deep relief, monograms, full case decoration.

I cut the metal myself. No laser, no transfer, no shortcut. Every line is a decision made once, with no undo.

Use it for: a family crest, a date that matters, a coat of arms, a single initial done properly.

---

### Department 2 — Skeletonisation

**Skeletonisation.**

Removing material from the movement until what's left is the watch's anatomy on display. Done well, it makes a familiar movement look like something you've never seen.

The brands skeletonise to a template. I skeletonise to a watch — yours. Bridge geometry, bevel angles, finishing style, what gets cut and what gets kept: all of it negotiable, all of it planned with you before the first chip comes off.

The hardest discipline in this list. Worth the wait.

---

### Department 3 — Dials

**Dials.**

Custom dial work — full replacement, hand-painted detail, lacquer, enamel, aventurine, meteorite, stone, mother-of-pearl, openworked, sub-dial reconfiguration, applied indices, custom typography.

If you can describe the surface, I can probably make it. If you can't describe it yet, we'll find it together.

A dial is the only part of the watch the wearer looks at every time. It deserves the most thinking.

---

### Department 4 — Gem-setting

**Gem-setting.**

Diamonds, sapphires, rubies, emeralds, custom-cut stones. Pavé, baguette, invisible, snow, channel, gypsy. Bezels, cases, dials, bracelets, crowns.

I set stones the way the high jewellery houses do — hand-pierced settings, no glue, every stone individually seated. Most "iced-out" watches you see online would not survive a year. Mine survive a lifetime, because they're built like jewellery, not like decoration.

---

### Department 5 — Sapphire cases

**Sapphire cases.**

Replacing the metal case of a watch with a case machined from a single block of synthetic sapphire. The result: a watch you can see through, with the movement and dial floating inside.

This is one of the hardest things to do well in modern watchmaking. Sapphire is harder than steel and shatters under the wrong pressure. Tolerances are measured in microns. Water resistance has to be re-engineered from scratch.

The Sapphire Daytona Box was ten of these in matched sets. **[See it →]**

---

### Department 6 — Restoration

**Restoration.**

Bringing a watch back to itself. Vintage Pateks, broken Daytonas, neglected Vacherons, watches that have been "serviced" by people who shouldn't have touched them.

Restoration is the opposite job to modification. The goal is invisibility — when it's done well, you can't see I was there. Original parts wherever possible. Period-correct techniques. Patina respected, not erased.

If you've inherited a watch and you don't know what to do with it, send it to me before you send it anywhere else.

---

### Department 7 — Finishing

**Finishing.**

The catch-all department for everything that makes a watch *feel* expensive in the hand. Anglage on bridges. Black-polish on screw heads. Brushing, satin, frosted, sandblasted, sunray, perlage. Re-cutting tired bevels. Polishing out a scratch without rounding the case.

Finishing is where most modification work falls down. The brands spend more on finishing than they admit. I spend more than they do.

## 4. CASE STUDY — THE SAPPHIRE DAYTONA BOX

### Page header

**The Sapphire Daytona Box.**

**[Hero image: the ten watches in the open transparent box, lit, no metal in sight.]**

A single private commission. Ten Rolex Daytonas, each in a one-piece sapphire case, each with a different custom dial, each accented with diamonds, presented as a matched set in a transparent presentation box machined from the same sapphire stock.

One client. **[CONFIRM: roughly when — I've written "completed in 2024"; replace with the real year]** *Completed in 2024.* **[CONFIRM: timeline — I've written "fourteen months"; correct if wrong]** *Fourteen months from first conversation to delivery.*

---

### The brief

The client owned the watches she wanted. What she didn't want was to keep wearing them.

Her line, the day we met:

> *"I just don't want to wear the same watch as my driver."*

Daytonas, on the wrist of someone who could buy any watch in the world, had stopped doing the one job a watch is meant to do — be hers, and not anyone else's. The brief was simple in shape and demanding in execution: take the ten Daytonas she already owned, and turn them into ten watches that nobody else on earth would ever wear.

---

### The decision

Sapphire cases were not the obvious answer. They were the *right* answer.

A custom dial alone changes how a watch looks. A sapphire case changes what a watch *is.* Light passes through the entire object. The movement is no longer a private thing tucked behind metal — it becomes the centrepiece. On a Daytona, with its column-wheel chronograph and vertical clutch, that's a movement that earns being shown.

Ten watches in metal would have been ten variations on a theme. Ten watches in sapphire became a set — a coherent commission that reads as one piece of work, not ten.

---

### The build

Each case was machined from a single block of synthetic sapphire. The process is unforgiving: sapphire is the second-hardest material on the Mohs scale, behind diamond, and it shatters under stress that steel would absorb without complaint. Tolerances are tighter than for any metal case I've worked with. Water resistance has to be re-engineered, because the gaskets that work in a steel case sit differently against sapphire.

Each dial was unique. The client and I worked through the ten together — meteorite, aventurine, **[CONFIRM: list of dial materials/styles — I've written a plausible mix; correct or replace]** *malachite, lapis, mother-of-pearl, hand-painted lacquer in two colourways, openworked with the movement visible through the dial face, hammered gold, and a fully diamond-paved face.* Each one had to read as part of the set without repeating.

Diamond accents were placed differently on each watch — bezel on some, indices on others, crown on a few — so that the set felt rhythmic rather than uniform.

The presentation box was machined from the same sapphire stock as the cases, lined in a single piece of black silk, with each watch sitting in a custom-cut bed.

---

### What almost went wrong

**[CONFIRM: war story — I've written a plausible one. If something else actually almost went wrong, swap it in. If you'd rather not include a war story at all, delete this whole block — but I'd argue strongly to keep it. Case studies without setbacks read as marketing.]**

*The seventh case cracked on final polishing.*

*Sapphire forgives nothing. A microscopic stress concentration, invisible until the polishing wheel finds it, was enough to lose a case I'd already spent six weeks on. The block of sapphire was already cut. The next block had a slightly different optical character and would not have matched the other nine.*

*I rang the supplier in Switzerland that night. New raw stock arrived inside a fortnight, cut from an adjacent slab to keep the optical match. I rebuilt the case from scratch and moved the rest of the schedule six weeks to the right.*

*The client knew on day one. She thanked me for telling her.*

---

### Why this commission matters

This is the kind of brief the brands cannot accept.

It isn't a question of money. It's a question of mandate. A manufacture exists to protect a model line. They cannot take a customer's Daytona, cut its case off, and rebuild it in sapphire — the watch would no longer be a Daytona in any sense the brand recognises. They would lose authorship of the object the moment the work began. So they say no.

I say yes, because saying yes is the entire point of what I do. The client kept her ten Daytonas. She also got ten watches that are hers in a way no production watch can be.

The driver still wears a Daytona. Hers no longer looks anything like it.
